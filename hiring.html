The Problem

Every new hire takes longer to ramp than the last one.

It's not that the people are getting worse. It's that the business hasn't documented what the good ones figured out on their own. So every new hire relearns the same lessons, the owner or the top performer becomes the de facto training program, and nothing scales.

You keep looking for "A-players" because your system only works with A-players. Your best people can't take a vacation without things slipping. Training is whoever is next to the new hire that week. Nobody writes anything down because "that's how we've always done it."

You can't promote from within because nobody knows how to train a replacement. And you can't hire ahead of demand because every new body is a drain on the people who already know what they're doing.

What I Install

Four deliverables. Yours to keep.

Michael Gerber called it the franchise prototype — the idea that a business should run the same way whether it's you behind the counter or someone you hired last month. That means documented roles, scripted training, clear measurement, and real rules — not heroics.

01
Role Scorecards

One-page scorecards for every seat in the business: mission, outcomes, competencies, weekly metrics. The document every hire reads before their first day. Most small businesses don't have this — which is why the first week of every new hire is someone else guessing what to give them.

02
Training Curriculum

A written, sequenced path from hired to independently productive for every role. Usually 30, 60, and 90-day milestones with shadowing windows, SOP reviews, and measurable checkpoints. The test: could someone else use this to onboard the next hire without you in the room?

03
SOP Library

The five to fifteen processes your business actually runs on, written down so a new hire can follow them without interrupting a senior team member. Documented the way your team actually works — not the way the software vendor wishes they worked.

04
Values as Vaccinations

Five behavioral rules — drawn from the failure modes you've personally seen — that every new hire learns on day one. Not aspirational values. Rules that protect the business from the mistakes you've already lived through.

How It Works

Build the business so ordinary people produce extraordinary results.

The system does the heavy lifting, not the individual. A structured 60-day engagement with real deliverables and a clear end state.

Step 1
Role Clarity

Every seat in the business gets a written scorecard: what the role is responsible for, what good looks like, what the weekly metrics are, who the role answers to. Most small businesses don't have this, which is why the first week of every new hire is someone else guessing what to give them.

Step 2
The Training Curriculum

A structured, repeatable path from "hired" to "independently productive" for every role. Usually a mix of written SOPs, shadowing windows, and measurable milestones. The test: could someone else use this to onboard the next hire without you in the room?

Step 3
The Scorecard System

A weekly rhythm where every hire sees the same three-to-five metrics about their own performance. Self-correction replaces manager correction. The best employees push themselves; the weak ones show up in the data before they show up in a customer complaint.
