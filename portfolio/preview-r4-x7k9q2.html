The Challenge

A coach with a program. No system to run it on.

Will Tams wanted to start a pickleball coaching business in the East Bay. He'd coached Ultimate Frisbee and baseball for 25 years, but pickleball coaching was new. He wanted to focus on what he's best at, the coaching, but everything else was pulling him off the court.

Signups were a Google spreadsheet. Payments were Venmo when people showed up. Half the time he didn't know who'd paid until the lesson started. Off-the-shelf SaaS didn't fit a small coaching business, and he had no in-house engineering. Every operational problem was his to solve, alone, between coaching sessions.

Then the first clinic sold out. Then the second. The pain points started compounding.

The Approach

Chuck came in as the consultant. Will stayed on the court.

Will brought me in to build the custom software his coaching business needed. He'd run Road to 4.0 and do the actual coaching. I'd build the platform underneath it, one pain point at a time.

No roadmap. No big design phase. Will would hit a wall on Tuesday (a refund chase, a no-show wave, a roster mismatch) and I'd ship a fix by the weekend. Every pain point became its own working session: what's actually broken, what would solve it, and what's the smallest version I can ship before the next clinic.

From hobby to business in one signup flow.

Before I got involved, signups lived in a spreadsheet and money lived in Venmo. Will would coach a clinic and then chase players off-court for payment, sometimes for days afterward. Half the people who said they'd come never paid. There was no way to actually reserve a spot.

It wasn't a business yet. It was a hobby with money attached.

The fix was the foundation: a real signup flow with Stripe Connect, where paying is reserving. No payment, no spot. Roster auto-built from confirmed payments. Refund policy enforced by code. The day this shipped, Road to 4.0 stopped being a hobby.

In pickleball, the math matters. You need clean sets of four. One no-show and your courts are off. One late arrival and the coach has to stop and catch them up, which means the players who showed up on time get less of the lesson they paid for.

Will couldn't babysit sixteen people's calendars every week. He needed the system to do it.

The fix was simple, and it worked. Every session got automated reminder emails: one 24 hours out, one 2 hours out. Players actually showed up. The ones who couldn't make it had time to refund or cancel cleanly. Everyone else got a real lesson.

Players sorted by skill, automatically.

Pickleball players really, really, really want to be matched by skill. When skill levels are mismatched, the better players get bored, the weaker players feel exposed, and the lesson feels off, even when everything else is working.

The very first drill group, sixteen people showed up and there was no way to sort them. Some were total beginners, some were edging toward 4.0. They all played on the same courts, in the same drills, and most went home thinking "that wasn't quite for me."

The fix had two pieces, working together: players self-rated their skill at signup, and the roster auto-sorted them onto courts by skill level. By clinic three, the problem was gone. Reviews went up. Retention went up. Players told their friends.

The Pain

"Beginners and advanced players on the same court. Nobody's getting the lesson they came for."

What I Built

Skill self-assessment on signup, plus a roster that auto-sorts by skill onto separate courts. Two pieces, one outcome: players matched correctly from the moment they walk on.

Reviews up
